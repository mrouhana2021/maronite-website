THE MARONITE CHURCH

An Ancient Eastern Tradition

Rooted in Antioch

The Maronite Church traces its origins to the followers of Saint Maron, a 4th-century Syrian hermit monk renowned for his holiness and miraculous gifts. His disciples formed a monastic community along the Orontes River in Syria, establishing what would become the Maronite spiritual and ecclesial tradition.

As one of the Eastern Catholic Churches in full communion with the Holy See of Rome, the Maronite Church retains its own liturgical rite, the Syriac-Maronite Antiochene Rite, celebrated primarily in Syriac and Arabic, with elements of the language Jesus himself spoke.

The Maronite Church has never had a period of separation from Rome — a source of great pride for Maronites worldwide and a witness to unity in the universal Church.

"The Maronite Church is the Church of Lebanon, and Lebanon is the message of the Maronite Church to the world — the message of coexistence, dialogue, and peace."
— MARONITE SPIRITUAL TRADITION

FOUNDATIONS

Pillars of Maronite Faith

01

Communion with Rome

The Maronite Church has always remained in full, unbroken communion with the successor of Saint Peter, a testament to our fidelity to the universal Church.

02

Syriac Liturgy

Our worship is celebrated in the Syriac-Antiochene rite, drawing on the language and spirituality of the earliest Christian communities in the East.

03

Monastic Spirit

Monastic life has always been the heartbeat of the Maronite Church, giving rise to great saints like Saint Charbel, Saint Rafqa, and Saint Nimatullah.

04

Lebanon as Message

The cedar-crowned land of Lebanon is inseparable from Maronite identity — a model of coexistence, dialogue, and witness for Christians in the Middle East.

WORSHIP

The Maronite Liturgy

قدّوس قدّوس قدّوس

Holy, Holy, Holy

The Maronite Qurbono (Divine Liturgy) is a feast of the senses and the spirit — ancient Syriac chants, fragrant incense, and the breaking of bread that has united the faithful across seventeen centuries. When you attend a Maronite Mass, you encounter a living link to the earliest Christian worship.
